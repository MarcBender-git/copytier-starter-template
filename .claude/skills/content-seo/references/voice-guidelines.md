# Voice Guidelines — Contractor Niches

## The Contractor Voice Framework

Every contractor website needs a voice that sits at a precise point on two axes:

```
FORMAL ◄────────────────────────────────► CASUAL
                      ▲
                 TARGET ZONE
              "Conversational Authority"
                      ▼
DISTANT ◄───────────────────────────────► PERSONAL
```

**Conversational Authority** means: the visitor feels like they're talking to a knowledgeable neighbor — not reading a corporate brochure, not texting a buddy. The contractor knows more than they do about this topic, communicates that clearly, and doesn't make them feel dumb for needing help.

### Universal Voice Rules (All Niches)

**Always:**
- Use "you" and "your" — write directly to one person
- Use the contractor's first name or "the team" for a human feel
- Acknowledge the visitor's situation before pitching the solution
- Be specific: "18 years" beats "years of experience"; "4.9 stars across 347 reviews" beats "highly rated"
- Use contractions ("we'll" not "we will," "you're" not "you are")
- State the guarantee/warranty explicitly and in plain English

**Never:**
- Use passive voice in headlines or CTAs
- Use corporate filler phrases ("we pride ourselves," "our passion is")
- Use superlatives without proof ("best," "top-rated," "leading")
- Speak down to the visitor or imply they caused the problem
- Use jargon without plain-English translation
- Use "at this time" or "currently" — just say what's true

## Niche Voice Profiles

### 🔧 Plumber

**Core Voice:** "Your reliable neighbor who happens to be an expert."

A good plumber voice is calm in an emergency. The customer is stressed — a burst pipe, no hot water, sewage backup. The copy acknowledges the urgency without amplifying the panic. It communicates: "This is solvable. We've fixed this exact problem hundreds of times. Here's what happens next."

**Tone descriptors:** Calm · Reliable · Direct · Reassuring · Specific

**Language that works:**
- "We'll have your water back on today."
- "You don't need to tear up the floor — we have cameras."
- "Licensed plumber, not a handyman."
- "We carry the parts on the truck."
- "No surprise charges. We quote before we wrench."

**Language to avoid:**
- "Plumbing solutions" (use: "plumbing repairs" or be specific)
- "Comprehensive plumbing services" (meaningless)
- "Your go-to plumber" (cliché)
- "We handle all your plumbing needs" (too vague)

**Urgency handling:** Match urgency to the situation. Burst pipe copy ≠ water softener installation copy. Dial urgency language to match the visitor's likely emotional state for that service page.

**Sample paragraph (emergency service):**
> "A burst pipe doesn't wait for business hours. Neither do we. [Business Name] responds to plumbing emergencies across Sacramento within [response time] — day, night, or weekends. We show up with the parts to fix it, not just diagnose it, because you need water flowing again, not a return trip."

**Sample paragraph (standard service):**
> "Water heater giving out? Before you decide between repair and replacement, let us take a look. We'll give you an honest answer — sometimes a $200 repair adds 5 more years to a unit. Other times, replacement makes more sense financially. We'll show you the math and let you decide."

### ❄️ HVAC (Heating & Air Conditioning)

**Core Voice:** "The trusted professional you call when comfort is non-negotiable."

HVAC customers feel one of two things: desperate (it's 105°F and the AC died) or cautious (planning a system replacement, concerned about cost). The voice shifts accordingly. Emergency tone = fast and confident. Replacement/installation tone = thorough, educational, trustworthy.

**Tone descriptors:** Competent · Efficient · Trustworthy · Explanatory · Confident

**Language that works:**
- "Same-day AC repair when the heat is on."
- "We don't just fix the symptom — we find the cause."
- "NATE-certified technicians." (always spell out what certification means)
- "Your home's comfort, not our commission."
- "We'll tell you when repair makes sense and when it doesn't."

**Language to avoid:**
- "HVAC solutions" (too vague)
- "All makes and models" (say which brands specifically if possible)
- "Climate control professionals" (say "heating and cooling technicians")
- Overselling urgency on non-emergency services

**Seasonal sensitivity:** AC copy in summer, heating copy in winter. If the site will serve year-round, lead with the season-relevant service on the homepage hero and use conditional logic if technically feasible.

**Sample paragraph (emergency):**
> "AC down in summer? We know that's not just uncomfortable — it's a health concern, especially for families with young kids or elderly relatives. [Business Name] offers same-day AC repair throughout [service area]. Call by noon, and we'll have a technician at your door today."

**Sample paragraph (system replacement):**
> "A new HVAC system is a 15–20 year decision. We're not going to rush you through it. We'll assess your home's square footage, insulation, and ductwork before recommending a system — because the right-sized unit saves you money on every energy bill for the next two decades."

### ⚡ Electrician

**Core Voice:** "The professional who keeps your home safe."

Electrical work carries a safety dimension that no other trade does quite as directly. The copy must acknowledge this without being alarmist. The visitor is often scared — electrical issues feel dangerous and mysterious. The voice should be grounding (literally and figuratively): calm, knowledgeable, safety-focused, and absolutely not DIY-encouraging.

**Tone descriptors:** Safety-focused · Methodical · Credentialed · Clear · Non-alarmist

**Language that works:**
- "Licensed electrician — not a risk worth skipping."
- "We'll find it. We'll fix it safely. We'll explain what happened."
- "Permitted work you can document for resale."
- "NEC code-compliant, every time."
- "We respect your home. We clean up after ourselves."

**Language to avoid:**
- Anything that implies DIY is a viable option for electrical work
- "Electrical solutions" (use specific service names)
- "Shocking prices" or any electrical pun (visitors don't find it reassuring)
- Overselling fear of electrical fires (acknowledge risk once, then focus on resolution)

**Sample paragraph:**
> "Flickering lights or a tripping breaker isn't just annoying — it's your home telling you something is wrong. [Business Name] diagnoses electrical issues properly, with the right equipment, by a licensed electrician. We don't guess. We trace the problem to the source and fix it with permitted work that protects your home's value."

### 🏠 Roofer

**Core Voice:** "The professional you trust with your biggest investment."

The roof is the most expensive single component most homeowners will ever repair or replace. The visitor is either in crisis (active leak, storm damage) or planning mode (aging roof, selling the house). Both require trust-building copy more than urgency copy. Roofing copy should feel substantial — this isn't a quick fix, it's a significant decision, and the copy should respect that.

**Tone descriptors:** Serious · Authoritative · Thorough · Warranty-focused · Trustworthy

**Language that works:**
- "Your roof should outlast your mortgage."
- "We document everything — before, during, and after."
- "We work directly with your insurance adjuster."
- "Manufacturer-certified installation. That's how you get the best warranty."
- "We'll tell you if you need a new roof. We'll also tell you if you don't."

**Language to avoid:**
- Storm-chasing language ("your neighbors are getting new roofs!")
- High-pressure replacement upsells when repair is appropriate
- Vague warranty claims ("lifetime warranty" — lifetime of what? of whom?)
- Technical roofing terms without plain-English translation

**Sample paragraph:**
> "After a storm, you need answers — not a sales pitch. [Business Name] provides free post-storm roof inspections with a detailed photo report. If your roof needs insurance-covered repairs, we handle the documentation and work directly with your adjuster. If it doesn't need work, we'll tell you that too. Our reputation is worth more than one job."

### 🌿 Landscaper / Lawn Care

**Core Voice:** "The reliable crew who makes your property look like you care."

Landscaping is aspirational for some customers (designing a dream backyard) and maintenance-focused for others (keeping the lawn presentable). The voice should match the service: design/install work gets warmer and more visual language; maintenance/lawn care gets clean, reliable, professional language.

**Tone descriptors:** Friendly · Visual · Reliable · Professional · Neighborhood-appropriate

**Language that works:**
- "Your neighbors will notice." (use sparingly, only when genuinely relevant)
- "Show up every week, same crew, same time."
- "Before/after photos in your project gallery."
- "We design around your lifestyle, not ours."
- "Licensed, insured, and familiar with [city] water restrictions."

**Language to avoid:**
- "Lush, vibrant landscapes" (generic)
- Excessive nature metaphors
- Overpromising on drought-tolerant landscaping results
- Ignoring water restrictions context for California sites

**Sample paragraph (maintenance):**
> "Reliable lawn care means showing up when we say we will — every week, same crew, same high cut or low, whatever you prefer. [Business Name] has maintained [City] properties since [year]. You shouldn't have to wonder if the crew is coming. You shouldn't have to leave notes. You should just see a clean lawn."

### 🔨 Handyman

**Core Voice:** "The competent, trustworthy person who gets the list done."

The handyman audience is often frustrated — they've tried to find a reliable handyman forever, been let down by flaky contractors, or have a growing list of small repairs they keep deferring. The voice must address this frustration directly: reliability, showing up, doing it right. Trust-building is paramount because the handyman industry has a reliability problem.

**Tone descriptors:** Dependable · Straightforward · Practical · Honest · Thorough

**Language that works:**
- "We show up. We do what we said. We charge what we quoted."
- "Bring your whole list — we'll knock it out."
- "Background-checked, licensed where required, insured always."
- "We don't do the 'we need to order a part and reschedule' thing."
- "Honest about what we can do and what requires a specialist."

**Language to avoid:**
- "Handyman services" repeated too many times (sounds circular)
- Implying any job is too small (handymen make their living on small jobs)
- Overselling expertise — handymen are generalists, not specialists, and that's their value
- Promising same-day on everything (handymen often book out)

**Sample paragraph:**
> "Got a list? Good. Bring it all — the door that won't close, the bathroom fan that rattles, the fence post that's been leaning since March. [Business Name] works through your punch list efficiently and charges honestly by the hour. One visit, multiple problems solved. No rescheduling, no 'we'll need to come back.'"

### 🧹 House Cleaner / Cleaning Service

**Core Voice:** "The trustworthy crew you're comfortable letting into your home."

Cleaning is deeply personal — you're inviting strangers into your most private space. The copy must address the trust barrier directly. Before any prospect books a cleaning, they need to believe the crew is honest, thorough, and respectful. Reliability and trust come before any mention of cleaning results.

**Tone descriptors:** Trustworthy · Thorough · Warm · Respectful · Detail-oriented

**Language that works:**
- "Background-checked, referenced, and insured — because it's your home."
- "Same cleaner every time, unless you prefer otherwise."
- "We use [eco-friendly/specific] products — safe for kids and pets."
- "We notice the little things: the light switch, the baseboard, the grout."
- "If something isn't right, we come back and fix it. No argument."

**Language to avoid:**
- "Spotless clean" or "sparkling" (overused)
- Implying the home is currently dirty
- "Our team of professionals" (too corporate for a trust-first category)
- Anything that makes the prospect feel judged about the state of their home

**Sample paragraph:**
> "Letting someone into your home requires real trust. [Business Name] earns it. Every cleaner on our team is background-checked, referenced by past clients, and covered by our liability insurance. You get the same cleaner each visit — because familiarity means fewer missed spots, not fewer standards."

## Reading This Guide in Practice

When starting a build, pull the matching niche profile and set it as the voice standard at the top of the content document. Then, for every page, ask: "Does this copy sound like it came from the niche profile's voice?" If not, revise before publishing.

The niche profiles above are starting points — a 20-year family-owned plumbing company sounds different from a 3-year solo operator who just went legit. Adjust for the specific contractor's personality, tenure, and story while staying within the niche voice boundaries.
